01 About Me

I work inside
the gap.

Most privacy problems are not legal problems. They are operational ones. A court order that nobody knows how to respond to, a vendor who cannot name their sub-processors, a product team that has no idea what personal data they're collecting.

I started in Japan, learning what it actually takes to make GDPR and CCPA work for companies navigating cross-border data obligations for the first time. What struck me was not the law itself—it was how few people understood what the law actually demanded operationally.

Coming back to India was not a step back. India's data protection landscape was about to get a lot more serious and I wanted to be inside that. At PwC my work sits between law and operations—court orders, breach escalations, vendor risk, and product privacy with engineering teams.

I also run Making Law Human, a project I started because I kept watching smart people freeze the moment they read a contract. Redesigning contracts and regulatory frameworks to be actually understandable is the work.

Making Law Human - Field Research

Stress tested Indian companies
before the DPDP Rules were released.

Weeks of direct conversations with founders, security leads, and vendors before the DPDP Rules were finalised. What I found was not non-compliance as exception. It was systematic.

18+
Websites with broken consent flows
0/5
Companies could meet the 72-hour breach window
3
System failure patterns across breach, consent and vendor accountability
01
Breach Response
5 companies. None could detect, verify, escalate, and notify a breach within 72 hours. One security lead: "forget reporting, we'd be lucky to confirm we were breached at all."
02
Consent Flows
Pre-checked boxes on 8 sites. Bundled consent terms on 10. One app demanded cookie consent before showing its homepage. None passed Section 6's free, specific, informed standard.
03
Vendor Transparency
15 vendors contacted. Answers were vague, outdated, or absent. Three admitted using sub-processors they "cannot fully disclose." Processor accountability was non-existent.
04
Data Mapping
20+ vendors could not tell me what personal data they collect or for what purpose. One showed me their database schema and called it "data mapping."
05
SDF Awareness
A health-tech startup and an ed-tech platform processing children's data had never heard "Significant Data Fiduciary." Neither knew about DPIAs, independent audits, or obligations to children's data.

"DPDP will not fail because the law is too strict.
It will fail because too many companies will start preparing
only after they've already been caught."

04 Cover Letter

Why Apple.
Why now.

Dear Hiring Manager,

In 2021, Facebook spent millions on full-page newspaper ads attacking Apple. Not over a patent. Not over market share. But because Apple had given users a single prompt: "Allow tracking?" and meant it. That moment taught me what privacy at scale actually looks like.

Funny thing about privacy law: you spend most of your time fixing problems that never had to exist. The data was collected before anyone asked if it should be. The vendor was hired before anyone checked their practices. The product launched before anyone ran an impact assessment. Then you try to untangle it.

I joined PwC to learn how privacy works at scale, and I did, faster than I expected. But the more embedded I got inside client problems, the more I kept thinking about Apple. Not Apple the brand. Apple the example of what happens when "privacy by design" stops being a compliance checkbox and becomes operational reality.
